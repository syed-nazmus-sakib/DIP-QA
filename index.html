1. Mechanical Power Transmission & Drives

1.1 Mechanical Power Transmission

Question: What do you mean by mechanical power transmission? Shortly explain some basic mechanical power drives.

Definition:

Mechanical power transmission is the process of transferring energy from a prime mover (like an electric motor or IC engine) to a driven machine. This process typically involves the modification of Torque (T), Rotational Speed (N), and Direction of Motion.

Basic Mechanical Power Drives:

  • Belt Drives: Flexible connectors used for long center distances; rely on friction.
  • Chain Drives: Positive drives using sprockets and chains; no slip, but noisy.
  • Gear Drives: Toothed wheels in mesh; most compact and efficient for constant velocity ratios.
  • Rope Drives: Used for transmitting large power over very long distances (e.g., elevators, cranes).

1.2 Involute Profile

Question: What is the Involute Profile? Why is it important?

Definition:

An involute profile is a curve traced by a point on a taut string as it unwinds from a circle (called the base circle). In gear design, the involute curve is used as the standard shape for gear teeth.

Why is it Important?

  • Constant Velocity Ratio: Involute gears maintain a constant angular velocity ratio regardless of small changes in center distance, satisfying the fundamental Law of Gearing.
  • Ease of Manufacturing: Involute teeth can be accurately produced using simple cutting tools (rack-type cutters).
  • Interchangeability: Gears with involute profiles of the same module and pressure angle can mesh correctly, making standardization possible.
  • Smooth Operation: The contact between involute teeth occurs along a straight line (line of action), ensuring smooth force transmission.

2. Chain Drives & Gear Tooth Profile

2.1 Chain Drive System

Question: Describe the relative advantages and disadvantages of chain drive system. Also mention its application.

Definition: A power transmission system using an endless chain wrapped around toothed wheels called sprockets. It combines the features of belt drives (flexibility) and gear drives (no slip).

Relative Advantages:

  • No Slip: Provides a positive drive with a constant velocity ratio.
  • High Efficiency: Transmits up to 98% of power with minimal friction loss.
  • Space Efficiency: Can transmit high torque in a smaller footprint compared to belt drives.
  • Center Distance Flexibility: Can be used for both short and long center distances.
  • Durability: Resistant to high temperatures, moisture, and oil (unlike rubber belts).
  • Low Bearing Load: Requires no initial tension, reducing the load on shafts and bearings.

Disadvantages:

  • Chordal Action: Causes slight fluctuations in velocity (vibration/pulsation) due to the polygonal shape of the sprocket.
  • Noise: Inherently noisier than belt drives, especially at high speeds.
  • Lubrication: Requires constant lubrication to prevent wear and "stretching" (pitch elongation).
  • Cost: Generally more expensive to manufacture and maintain than flat or V-belts.
  • Precision: Not suitable for high-precision motion where backlash must be zero.

Common Applications:

  • Transportation: Bicycles, motorcycles, and automotive timing chains.
  • Industrial: Conveyor belts in factories and assembly lines.
  • Heavy Machinery: Agricultural equipment (tractors), forklifts, and construction cranes.

2.2 Spur Gear Tooth Profile

Question: Draw the profile of a spur gear tooth and provide its nomenclature.

A spur gear is the simplest type of gear, where the teeth are cut parallel to the axis of the shaft.

Key Nomenclature (Structured Definitions):

TermDefinition

Dedendum ($h_f$)The radial distance from the pitch circle to the bottom of the tooth space.

3. Shaft Design & Bearings

3.1 Shaft Material Selection & Design

Question: What are the criteria of material selection of shaft? Briefly describe different aspects of shaft design.

Criteria for Material Selection:

A shaft must withstand high stresses and transmit power with minimal deflection. The material is selected based on:

  • High Strength: To resist tension, compression, and shear stresses.
  • Machinability: To easily cut keyways, splines, and threads.
  • Low Notch Sensitivity: To prevent fatigue failure at points of stress concentration (like shoulders).
  • Hardenability: To allow heat treatment for increased wear resistance at bearing surfaces.
  • High Modulus of Elasticity (E): To minimize elastic deflection and maintain alignment.

Common Materials: Carbon steels (40C8, 45C8) for general use; Alloy steels (Nickel, Chromium, Molybdenum) for high-strength requirements.

Aspects of Shaft Design:

1. Strength Design:

  • Torsional Loads: Designing against the torque ($T$) required to transmit power.
  • Bending Loads: Accounting for the weight of gears/pulleys and belt tensions.
  • Combined Loading: Most shafts face both; we use Equivalent Twisting Moment ($T_e$) and Equivalent Bending Moment ($M_e$).
  • Axial Loads: Dealing with thrust from helical or bevel gears.

2. Rigidity (Stiffness) Design:

  • Lateral Rigidity: Ensuring the shaft doesn't "bow" too much, which would cause gear misalignment.
  • Torsional Rigidity: Limiting the "twist" angle ($\theta$) per unit length to prevent timing errors or vibrations.

4. Pneumatic, Hydraulic & Coupling Systems

4.1 Pneumatic Drive & Hydraulic Systems

Question: Draw the block diagram of a typical pneumatic drive system. Also mention some advantages and disadvantages of hydraulic systems.

Pneumatic Drive Block Diagram:

A typical pneumatic system converts the energy of compressed air into mechanical motion.

  1. Compressor: Draws in atmospheric air and compresses it.
  2. Reservoir (Air Tank): Stores compressed air to smooth out pressure fluctuations.
  3. FRL Unit (Filter, Regulator, Lubricator): Cleans the air, sets the working pressure, and adds oil mist for lubrication.
  4. Control Valves: Directs the flow of air to the desired path.
  5. Actuator (Cylinder/Motor): Converts air pressure into linear or rotary work.

Hydraulic Systems: Pros and Cons

Advantages:

  • High Power Density: Can move extremely heavy loads with small actuators.
  • Incompressibility: Provides precise, "stiff" motion control without the "sponginess" of air.
  • Self-Lubricating: The hydraulic fluid itself lubricates the internal components.

Disadvantages:

  • Leakage Risk: Messy and can pose environmental or fire hazards.
  • Maintenance: Requires high-precision seals and frequent oil filtration.
  • Speed: Slower than pneumatic systems due to fluid viscosity.

4.2 Flexible Couplings

Question: Briefly describe the types and functions of flexible couplings used in industrial power transmission systems.

Function: Couplings connect two shafts to transmit torque. Flexible versions are specifically designed to permit slight misalignment (angular, parallel, or axial) and absorb shocks/vibration.

Common Types:

  • Bush-Pin Type Flange Coupling: Uses rubber or leather bushes over pins to provide flexibility and dampen vibrations.
  • Oldham Coupling: Used for connecting shafts with lateral (parallel) misalignment. It consists of two slotted hubs and a central floating disc.
  • Universal (Hooke's) Joint: Used when shafts intersect at a large angle (e.g., automotive propeller shafts).
  • Gear Coupling: Uses internal and external teeth to transmit high torque while allowing for minor misalignment.

4.3 Torque Converter & Transmission

Question: What do you mean by torque converter? Differentiate between manual and automatic transmission.

Torque Converter Definition:

A fluid coupling used in automatic transmissions to transfer rotating power from the engine to the transmission. It allows the engine to spin somewhat independently of the transmission (allowing the car to stop while in gear) and acts as a torque multiplier during acceleration.

Manual vs. Automatic Transmission:

FeatureManual TransmissionAutomatic Transmission
Coupling DeviceUses a Friction Clutch.Uses a Torque Converter.
Gear SelectionDriver manually selects gears via a lever and clutch pedal.ECU/Hydraulics select gears based on speed and load.
Power FlowDirect mechanical link; very efficient.Fluid-based link; historically less efficient.
ControlFull driver control; better for heavy towing or racing.Easier for the driver; ideal for heavy traffic.
ComplexitySimple construction, lower maintenance.Highly complex with planetary gears and hydraulic logic.

5. Gear Trains

5.1 Gear Train Types

Question: What is a gear train? Differentiate between different types of gear trains.

Definition: A Gear Train is a combination of two or more gears used to transmit motion and power from one shaft to another. They are used when a large speed reduction is required in a small space or when the distance between the input and output shafts is too great for a single pair of gears.

Classification of Gear Trains:

TypeDescriptionKey Characteristic
Simple Gear TrainEach shaft carries only one gear.Used for short center distances; idler gears change direction but not velocity ratio.
Compound Gear TrainAt least one shaft carries two or more gears that rotate at the same speed.Allows for very high speed reduction (V.R.) in a compact space.
Reverted Gear TrainThe axes of the first and last gears are co-axial (collinear).Common in industrial gearboxes and clocks where input/output must align.
Epicyclic Gear TrainOne or more gear axes move (rotate) relative to another axis (using a "carrier" or "arm").Extremely high torque/speed ratios; used in differentials and automatic transmissions.

Detailed Comparison:

1. Simple Gear Train: The speed ratio depends only on the number of teeth on the driving and driven gears. Velocity Ratio (V.R.): $N_1/N_2 = T_2/T_1$. Idler Gears do not affect the V.R. but help bridge distances and change direction.

2. Compound Gear Train: Intermediate shafts have two gears keyed together. Much higher gear ratios can be achieved compared to simple trains.

3. Reverted Gear Train: Input and output shafts lie on the same straight line. Used in automotive transmissions and lathe back gears.

4. Epicyclic (Planetary) Gear Train: Consists of a Sun gear, Planet gears, a Planet Carrier (Arm), and often an internal Ring gear (Annulus). It has two degrees of freedom. Tabular Method is essential for exam problems.

6. Cam & Follower Mechanisms

6.1 Radial vs. Offset Follower & Cam Nomenclature

Question: Differentiate between radial and offset follower. Show the nomenclature of a cam using an appropriate diagram.

Radial vs. Offset Follower:

FeatureRadial FollowerOffset Follower
AlignmentThe centerline of the follower passes exactly through the center of rotation of the cam.The centerline of the follower is displaced by a distance $x$ (offset) from the cam's center.
Side ThrustHigher side thrust during the rise stroke.Reduced side thrust during the working stroke (rise), leading to smoother motion.
Pressure AngleGenerally higher for a given cam size.The offset helps in decreasing the maximum pressure angle during the rise.
ApplicationStandard applications where space is symmetrical.High-speed engines or precision machinery where reducing lateral force is critical.

Nomenclature of a Cam:

  • Base Circle: The smallest circle that can be drawn tangent to the cam profile from the center of rotation. It dictates the overall size of the cam.
  • Trace Point: A theoretical point on the follower used to generate the pitch curve.
  • Pitch Curve: The path generated by the trace point as the follower moves relative to the cam.
  • Prime Circle: The smallest circle drawn from the cam center tangent to the pitch curve.
  • Pressure Angle ($\alpha$): The angle between the normal to the pitch curve and the direction of follower motion. Should be kept small (usually $<30^\circ$) to prevent jamming.
  • Pitch Point: The point on the pitch curve where the pressure angle is at its maximum.
  • Stroke (Lift): The maximum travel of the follower from its lowest position to its highest position.
  • Dwell: The period during which the cam rotates but the follower remains stationary at a specific height.

7. Belt Drives

7.1 Slip and Creep in Belt Drives

Question: What are the slip and the creep of a belt drive? How do they affect the performance of a belt drive?

1. Slip in Belt Drives:

  • Definition: Slip is the actual sliding of the belt over the pulley surface. It occurs when the difference in tension ($T_1 - T_2$) exceeds the frictional grip.
  • Cause: Insufficient initial tension, overloading the drive, or a small coefficient of friction.
  • Result: Reduction in Velocity Ratio; Heat Generation; rapid belt wear.
  • Formula: $V.R. = \frac{N_2}{N_1} = \frac{d_1}{d_2}\left(1 - \frac{s}{100}\right)$, where $s$ is the total percentage slip.

2. Creep in Belt Drives:

  • Definition: Creep is the elastic deformation of the belt as it passes from the tight side (high tension, $T_1$) to the slack side (low tension, $T_2$).
  • Mechanism: On the Tight Side, the belt is stretched. On the Slack Side, the belt contracts. This continuous stretching and contracting causes a "creeping" motion.
  • Cause: The elastic property (Young's Modulus) of the belt material. It is an inherent physical phenomenon and cannot be completely eliminated.

Performance Impacts:

FeatureImpact of SlipImpact of Creep
Power LossHigh; energy is lost as heat.Minimal; energy is lost in internal molecular friction.
Velocity RatioSignificant drop; becomes unpredictable.Slight reduction (usually < 1%).
Wear & TearSevere surface abrasion on the belt.Minor internal fatigue over time.
ControlCan be reduced by increasing tension or using "Belt Dressing."Dependent on material elasticity; cannot be stopped.

Combined Formula:

$$N_2 = N_1 \times \frac{d_1}{d_2} \times \left(1 - \frac{s}{100}\right) \times \left[\frac{E + \sigma_2}{E + \sigma_1}\right]$$

(Where $E$ is Young's Modulus and $\sigma$ is stress in the belt.)

9. Chain Drive & Spur Gear (Detailed)

9.1 Chain Drive Advantages & Disadvantages

Question: Describe the relative advantages and disadvantages of chain drive. Also mention its application.

Definition: A chain drive consists of an endless chain whose links mesh with toothed wheels called sprockets. It is an intermediate drive type that offers the flexibility of a belt but the "positive" engagement of a gear.

Relative Advantages:

  • No Slip: Since it is a positive drive, it maintains a constant velocity ratio.
  • High Efficiency: Transmits power with efficiency up to 98% under ideal conditions.
  • Compactness: Can transmit higher torque in a smaller space compared to belt drives.
  • Lower Bearing Load: Unlike belts, chains do not require high initial tension, reducing the load on shafts and bearings.
  • Environmental Resistance: Can operate in high temperatures or oily environments where rubber belts would fail.

Disadvantages:

  • Velocity Fluctuations: Known as Chordal Action, the chain moves in a polygonal path rather than a perfect circle, causing slight speed variations.
  • High Noise: Inherently noisier than belts, especially at high speeds.
  • Lubrication Requirement: Requires constant lubrication to prevent wear and "elongation" of the chain pitch.
  • Cost: Generally more expensive to manufacture than flat or V-belts.

Applications:

  • Transportation: Bicycles, motorcycles, and automotive timing systems.
  • Industrial: Conveyors, rolling mills, and mining machinery.
  • Agricultural: Tractors and harvesting equipment.

10. Cam Mechanisms

10.1 Cam Definition & Applications

Question: What is cam? Write down at least three applications of cam.

Definition: A Cam is a rotating or reciprocating mechanical element that gives a predetermined oscillating, reciprocating, or stepping motion to another element called the follower. It serves as a method of converting rotary motion into complex linear motion.

Applications of Cams:

  1. Internal Combustion Engines: To operate the intake and exhaust valves at precise intervals (using the camshaft).
  2. Automatic Lathe Machines: To control the movement of cutting tools and the feeding of workpieces.
  3. Printing Machinery: To synchronize the movement of paper-feeding mechanisms and ink rollers.
  4. Textile Industry: Used in weaving machines to control the intricate movement of the loom.

10.2 Classification of Followers

Question: Classify followers according to surface in contact and show each of them with neat sketches.

Followers are primarily classified by the shape of the part that touches the cam profile.

1. Knife-Edge Follower:

  • Description: The contacting end is a sharp wedge.
  • Characteristic: Rarely used in heavy machinery because the small contact area leads to excessive wear.
  • Application: Precision instruments or low-speed mechanisms.

2. Roller Follower:

  • Description: A small roller is attached to the end of the follower.
  • Characteristic: Replaces sliding friction with rolling friction, significantly reducing wear and tear. Most widely used follower type.
  • Application: Automotive engines and high-speed industrial machinery.

3. Flat-Faced (Mushroom) Follower:

  • Description: The contacting end is a perfectly flat surface.
  • Characteristic: Can handle high-speed operation and is less sensitive to alignment errors, but produces high side thrust.
  • Application: Valve-actuating mechanisms in automobile engines.

4. Spherical-Faced Follower:

  • Description: The contacting end is machined into a spherical shape.
  • Characteristic: A modification of the flat-faced follower to reduce high surface stresses and avoid "digging" of edges into the cam.
  • Application: Specific heavy-duty machinery where high loads are expected.

11. Transmission Shaft & Kinematic Pairs

11.1 Transmission Shaft Functions & Material Properties

Question: What are the functions of transmission shaft? Also mention the required properties of shaft materials.

Definition: A transmission shaft is a rotating machine element, usually circular in cross-section, which is used to transmit power from a source (like a motor or engine) to a machine that absorbs power.

Functions of a Transmission Shaft:

  • Power Transmission: The primary role is to transfer torque from the driving unit to the driven unit.
  • Support for Components: It acts as a mounting axis for mechanical elements such as gears, pulleys, sprockets, and clutches.
  • Motion Synchronization: It ensures that all components keyed or splined to it rotate at the same angular velocity.
  • Resisting Loads: It must withstand the bending moments caused by the weight of these components and the lateral forces from belt tensions or gear meshing.

Required Properties of Shaft Materials:

  • High Strength: To resist high torsional and bending stresses without failure.
  • Good Machinability: Essential for cutting precise keyways, splines, and threads.
  • Low Notch Sensitivity: To prevent fatigue cracks from forming at stress concentration points.
  • High Modulus of Elasticity ($E$): To ensure high rigidity and minimize elastic deflection.
  • Wear Resistance: Particularly important for the surfaces that rotate within bearings.

Common Materials: Carbon steels (40C8, 45C8) for general use; Alloy steels (Nickel-Chromium-Molybdenum) for high-performance or heavy-duty shafts.

11.2 Kinematic Pair Classification

Question: Define kinematic pair. Classify kinematic pairs according to the type of contact between the elements and briefly describe each of them.

Definition: A Kinematic Pair is a joint between two links (or elements) of a machine that are in contact with each other and allow for relative motion. If the motion between the two links is completely or successfully constrained, they form a kinematic pair.

Classification According to Type of Contact:

TypeDescriptionExamples
Lower PairThe two elements have surface contact while in relative motion. The surface of one element slides over the other.Sliding pair (Piston in cylinder), Turning pair (Shaft in a journal bearing).
Higher PairThe two elements have point or line contact while in relative motion. The motion is often a combination of sliding and rolling.Cams and followers, Gears in mesh, Ball and roller bearings.

Brief Description:

  • Lower Pairs: Since the pressure is distributed over a large surface area, these are robust and better suited for heavy loads. They are generally easier to lubricate and have a longer life under high-stress conditions.
  • Higher Pairs: These allow for more complex motion profiles (like those in a cam). However, because the contact area is theoretically zero (a point or a line), they experience very high local contact stresses (Hertzian stress) and require higher manufacturing precision and hardened materials.

12. Gear Trains (Detailed)

12.1 Gear Train Types

Question: Define gear train. Shortly explain different types of gear train with appropriate figures.

Definition: A Gear Train is a combination of two or more gears used to transmit motion and power from one shaft to another. They are typically used when a large speed reduction is required in a small space.

Types of Gear Trains:

  • Simple Gear Train: Each shaft carries only one gear. The intermediate gears (idlers) change the direction of rotation but do not affect the overall velocity ratio. Used for short center distances.
  • Compound Gear Train: At least one shaft carries two or more gears keyed together so they rotate at the same speed. Allows for very high speed reduction or increase in a compact space.
  • Reverted Gear Train: A compound gear train where the input and output shafts lie on the same straight line. Common in automotive transmissions and clock mechanisms.
  • Epicyclic (Planetary) Gear Train: One or more gear axes move (rotate) relative to another axis using a "carrier" or "arm." Extremely high torque/speed ratios; used in automotive differentials and automatic transmissions.

12.2 Power Transmission Efficiency

Question: Why power transmission efficiency is not 100%? Mention some ways to increase this efficiency.

In any mechanical system, the output power is always less than the input power ($P_{out} < P_{in}$). Efficiency is never 100% due to the following reasons:

Why Efficiency is Not 100%:

  • Friction: The primary cause. Sliding friction between gear teeth, belt-on-pulley friction, and internal friction in bearings converts kinetic energy into heat.
  • Slippage: In non-positive drives like belts, "slip" and "creep" cause a loss of rotational speed.
  • Air Resistance (Windage): At high speeds, the rotation of components creates air turbulence and drag.
  • Hysteresis/Deformation: The continuous elastic stretching and contraction of belts or the slight deflection of gear teeth consumes energy.
  • Lubricant Viscosity: The "churning" of oil in a gearbox (oil drag) requires a portion of the input torque.

Ways to Increase Efficiency:

  • Proper Lubrication: Using the correct grade of oil/grease reduces the coefficient of friction and helps dissipate heat.
  • Precision Alignment: Ensuring shafts are perfectly parallel or intersecting minimizes uneven loading and unnecessary friction.
  • Use of Rolling Elements: Replacing sliding contact (journal bearings) with rolling contact (ball or roller bearings).
  • Material Selection: Using materials with high surface hardness and low friction coefficients.
  • Regular Maintenance: Replacing worn-out belts or gears that have lost their ideal profiles.
  • Surface Finish: Improving the surface finish of gear teeth (e.g., through grinding or honing).

13. Clutch & Brake Systems

13.1 Single Plate Diaphragm Clutch

Question: Briefly describe the functions and working principle of a single plate diaphragm clutch with diagram.

Definition: A single plate diaphragm clutch is a type of friction clutch that uses a diaphragm spring (a conical-shaped spring steel plate) instead of multiple helical coil springs to apply pressure to the friction plate.

Working Principle:

The clutch operates on the principle of friction between two surfaces. It has two states:

Engaged Position (Normal State):

  • The diaphragm spring is in its natural conical shape, exerting high axial pressure on the pressure plate.
  • This clamps the clutch plate (friction disc) firmly between the flywheel and the pressure plate.
  • Power is transmitted from the engine crankshaft to the gearbox input shaft via the splined hub of the clutch plate.

Disengaged Position (Pedal Pressed):

  • The driver pushes the clutch pedal, moving the release bearing forward against the center of the diaphragm spring.
  • The spring pivots on its fulcrum rings, causing its outer rim to move backward.
  • This pulls the pressure plate away from the clutch plate, creating a gap. The friction link is broken.

Functions:

  • Gradual Engagement: Allows the vehicle to start from rest without jerking by slipping initially.
  • Power Disconnection: Permits gear shifting by temporarily decoupling the engine from the transmission.
  • Compact Design: The diaphragm spring requires less space and provides more uniform pressure than coil springs.
  • Balanced Force: As friction lining wears, the diaphragm spring maintains a more constant clamping force.

13.2 Internal Expanding Brakes

Question: What do you mean by internal expanding brakes?

Definition: An internal expanding brake consists of two semi-circular brake shoes fitted with friction linings, housed inside a rotating brake drum. The shoes are anchored at one end and forced outward against the inner surface of the drum at the other end to create braking torque.

Core Mechanism:

  • Actuation: When the brake is applied (via a hydraulic cylinder or mechanical cam), the shoes expand radially outward.
  • Friction Generation: The friction between the stationary linings and the rotating drum converts the kinetic energy of the vehicle into heat.
  • Self-Energizing Effect: Depending on the direction of rotation, one shoe (the leading shoe) is pulled into the drum by the frictional force itself, increasing the braking pressure automatically. The other is called the trailing shoe.
